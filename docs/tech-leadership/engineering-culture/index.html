Engineering Culture

Engineering culture is the set of shared values, practices, and norms that define how an engineering organization operates. Great culture does not happen by accident â€” it is intentionally built and continuously maintained by technical leaders at every level. This page covers the essential cultural practices that distinguish high-performing engineering organizations.

Code Review Culture

Code reviews are one of the most impactful practices in software engineering. They catch bugs, spread knowledge, maintain standards, and build team cohesion. But their effectiveness depends entirely on culture.

Reviewer behaviors:

Automating the Tedious Parts

Blameless Postmortems

When things go wrong in production, the response should be learning, not blame. Blameless postmortems are a cornerstone of high-reliability engineering organizations.

Why Blameless?

  • People who fear punishment will hide mistakes and avoid reporting incidents
  • The root cause is almost always systemic, not individual
  • Blame shuts down the psychological safety needed for honest analysis
  • The goal is to fix the system, not to find a scapegoat

Postmortem Template

Running an Effective Postmortem Meeting

  1. Set the tone â€” â€œWe are here to learn, not to blame. The system failed, not a person.â€
  2. Build the timeline collaboratively â€” Walk through events chronologically
  3. Ask â€œwhyâ€ five times â€” Dig into root causes, not surface symptoms
  4. Focus on systemic fixes â€” â€œHow do we prevent ANY engineer from making this mistake?â€
  5. Assign action items with owners and deadlines â€” Postmortems without action items are theater
  6. Follow up â€” Track completion of action items; review in the next team meeting

Postmortem Anti-Patterns

  • Skipping the postmortem because â€œwe already know what happenedâ€
  • Writing postmortems that nobody reads (publish them prominently)
  • Action items that never get completed (track them like any other work)
  • Only doing postmortems for severe incidents (minor incidents have lessons too)
  • The â€œfive whysâ€ leading to â€œhuman errorâ€ â€” keep going until you find a systemic cause
